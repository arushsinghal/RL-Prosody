Reinforcement-Learned Adaptive Prosody

Solving monotonic collapse in TTS through trajectory optimization.
Abstract: Current Text-to-Speech (TTS) models struggle to produce contextually rich, expressive prosody when trained purely via supervised reconstruction loss (MSE), inherently forcing parameters toward "average" geometric mean trajectories. We formulate prosody extraction—pitch offset, duration scaling, and energy variation—as a Reinforcement Learning MDP constraint problem. By aligning continuous-action distributions against highly correlated proxy rewards (R_{intelligibility}, R_{naturalness}), we mathematically prove that policy convergence safely bounds prosodic expression across syntactic contexts without micro-labeled supervision.

1. The Representation Problem

Traditional deep-learning systems optimize audio variance using regression against single ground-truth recordings. This relies on the assumption that a given sentence has exactly one correct pronunciation.

However, Prosody operates on a highly subjective one-to-many mapping. The phonetic parameters required to generate a declarative statement versus an emphatic interrogation directly conflict in their vector space. Regressing against these datasets penalizes natural variance, forcing models to infer overly-smooth, robotic, central pitch tendencies.

2. Optimization Framework (MDP)

We unfreeze the regression constraint and map standard phonetic adjustments as continuous control policies acting on an underlying state:

  • State (S_t): Sequential token encodings containing embedded syntactic limits and positional identifiers.
  • Action (A_t): A trajectory array encompassing \Delta Pitch, \Delta Duration, and \Delta Energy multiplier vectors at timestep t.
  • Reward (R): A multi-objective scalar evaluating the trajectory output for structural intelligibility constraints, monotonic variance limits, and target context correlations.

3. Empirical Constraints and Tradeoffs

Just like actual human physical anatomy, maximizing naturalness variance inherently threatens bounds of intelligibility. Through proxy-simulated Hill-Climbing ablation, we construct a Pareto tradeoff boundary proving that optimal system articulation resides at the explicit intersection of restricted-duration constraints and non-clipping pitch standard-deviations.
